DIABETES — PATIENT GUIDE
=========================
Source: Adapted from MedlinePlus Health Topics (medlineplus.gov)
Topic Category: Chronic Disease Management
Last Reviewed: 2025

1. WHAT IS DIABETES?
---------------------
Diabetes is a chronic disease in which blood glucose (blood sugar) levels are too high. Glucose comes from the foods you eat. Insulin, a hormone made by the pancreas, helps glucose get into your cells to be used for energy.

There are three main types of diabetes:
- Type 1 Diabetes: The body does not make insulin. The immune system attacks and destroys the cells in the pancreas that make insulin. Type 1 diabetes is usually diagnosed in children and young adults.
- Type 2 Diabetes: The body does not make or use insulin well. This is the most common type of diabetes, accounting for 90-95% of all cases. It develops over many years and is usually diagnosed in adults, though it increasingly affects children and teens.
- Gestational Diabetes: A form of diabetes that develops during pregnancy. It usually goes away after the baby is born but increases the risk of Type 2 diabetes later in life.

2. SYMPTOMS OF DIABETES
------------------------
Common symptoms include:
- Frequent urination, especially at night
- Excessive thirst
- Unexplained weight loss
- Extreme hunger
- Blurry vision
- Numbness or tingling in hands or feet
- Fatigue and lack of energy
- Sores that heal slowly
- Very dry skin
- More infections than usual

Some people with Type 2 diabetes have no symptoms at all and are only diagnosed through routine blood tests.

3. DIAGNOSIS
-------------
Diabetes is diagnosed through blood tests:
- Fasting Blood Sugar Test: Blood is drawn after an 8-hour fast. Normal is below 100 mg/dL. Diabetes is diagnosed at 126 mg/dL or above.
- A1C Test: Measures average blood sugar over the past 2-3 months. Normal is below 5.7%. Diabetes is diagnosed at 6.5% or above.
- Oral Glucose Tolerance Test: Blood is tested 2 hours after drinking a glucose-containing drink.
- Random Blood Sugar Test: Blood is tested at any time. Diabetes is suspected at 200 mg/dL or above with symptoms.

4. TREATMENT AND MANAGEMENT
-----------------------------
Diabetes management involves:

Lifestyle Changes (all types):
  - Healthy eating: Focus on vegetables, whole grains, lean protein, and healthy fats
  - Physical activity: Aim for 150 minutes of moderate exercise per week
  - Weight management: Even modest weight loss improves blood sugar control
  - Smoking cessation: Smoking worsens diabetes complications

Medications:
  - Type 1: Insulin therapy is required (injections or insulin pump)
  - Type 2: Oral medications (metformin is usually first-line), followed by other agents if needed
  - Some Type 2 patients also require insulin
  - GLP-1 receptor agonists and SGLT-2 inhibitors are newer classes with proven heart and kidney benefits

Blood Sugar Monitoring:
  - Check blood sugar as directed by your provider (may be multiple times daily)
  - Continuous Glucose Monitors (CGM) are available and recommended for many patients
  - Target blood sugar levels are individualized by your provider

5. TARGET BLOOD SUGAR LEVELS (Typical Ranges)
----------------------------------------------
- Before meals: 80-130 mg/dL
- Two hours after meals: Less than 180 mg/dL
- A1C goal: Less than 7% for most adults (individualized by provider)
Note: Your provider will set personalized targets based on your age, health status, and risk factors.

6. COMPLICATIONS OF UNCONTROLLED DIABETES
-------------------------------------------
If blood sugar remains too high for too long, serious complications can develop:
- Heart disease and stroke (leading causes of death in people with diabetes)
- Kidney disease (diabetic nephropathy) — leading cause of kidney failure
- Eye damage (diabetic retinopathy) — leading cause of adult blindness
- Nerve damage (diabetic neuropathy) — causes pain, numbness, and weakness
- Foot problems — poor circulation and nerve damage can lead to amputations
- Dental disease — increased risk of gum disease
- Sexual health problems

7. PREVENTING COMPLICATIONS
-----------------------------
Regular monitoring and preventive care are essential:
- See your primary care provider every 3-6 months
- Check your A1C at least twice per year (4 times if poorly controlled)
- Annual eye exam with a dilated eye examination
- Annual foot exam by your provider; daily self-examination at home
- Annual urine test for kidney function (urine albumin)
- Blood pressure monitoring at every visit (target: below 130/80)
- Annual dental checkup
- Vaccinations: Flu shot yearly; pneumonia, hepatitis B, and COVID-19 vaccines as recommended

8. LOW BLOOD SUGAR (HYPOGLYCEMIA)
-----------------------------------
Hypoglycemia occurs when blood sugar drops below 70 mg/dL.

Symptoms: Shakiness, sweating, dizziness, fast heartbeat, hunger, blurred vision, confusion, irritability

Treatment — The "Rule of 15":
  1. Eat or drink 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrates (4 glucose tablets, 4 oz juice, or 4 oz regular soda)
  2. Wait 15 minutes and recheck blood sugar
  3. If still below 70 mg/dL, repeat the treatment
  4. Once blood sugar is above 70 mg/dL, eat a small snack if your next meal is more than 1 hour away

Severe hypoglycemia (unconscious patient):
  - Do NOT give food or drink by mouth
  - Administer glucagon if available and trained
  - Call 911 immediately

9. HIGH BLOOD SUGAR (HYPERGLYCEMIA)
-------------------------------------
Symptoms: Increased thirst and urination, headache, blurred vision, fatigue

Causes: Too much food, too little medication, illness, stress, inactivity

What to do:
  - Check your blood sugar
  - Follow your sick-day rules if ill
  - Drink plenty of water
  - Take your diabetes medications as prescribed
  - Contact your provider if blood sugar remains high (over 300 mg/dL) or if you feel very sick

10. LIVING WELL WITH DIABETES
-------------------------------
- Diabetes education classes can help you manage your condition effectively
- Join a support group to connect with others managing diabetes
- Keep all scheduled appointments even when you feel well
- Share your diabetes diagnosis with close family members and coworkers
- Wear a medical ID bracelet identifying you as a person with diabetes
- Store insulin and medications properly as directed
