HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE (HYPERTENSION) — PATIENT GUIDE
====================================================
Source: Adapted from MedlinePlus Health Topics (medlineplus.gov)
Topic Category: Cardiovascular Health
Last Reviewed: 2025

1. WHAT IS HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE?
---------------------------------
High blood pressure, also called hypertension, is a condition in which the force of blood against your artery walls is consistently too high. Blood pressure is measured using two numbers:
- Systolic pressure (top number): The pressure when your heart beats
- Diastolic pressure (bottom number): The pressure when your heart rests between beats
Blood pressure is written as systolic/diastolic (e.g., 120/80 mmHg).

Blood Pressure Categories:
- Normal: Less than 120/80 mmHg
- Elevated: 120-129 / less than 80 mmHg
- Stage 1 Hypertension: 130-139 / 80-89 mmHg
- Stage 2 Hypertension: 140 or higher / 90 or higher mmHg
- Hypertensive Crisis (seek emergency care): Higher than 180 / higher than 120 mmHg

2. WHY HYPERTENSION IS DANGEROUS
-----------------------------------
Hypertension is often called the "silent killer" because most people have no symptoms. Over time, uncontrolled high blood pressure causes damage to blood vessels and organs, leading to:
- Heart attack and heart failure
- Stroke
- Kidney disease and kidney failure
- Vision loss and blindness
- Sexual dysfunction
- Peripheral artery disease
- Aneurysm (dangerous bulging of a blood vessel)

3. RISK FACTORS
----------------
Non-modifiable risk factors (cannot be changed):
- Age (risk increases with age)
- Family history of hypertension
- Race (African Americans develop high blood pressure more often and at a younger age)
- Chronic kidney disease

Modifiable risk factors (can be changed):
- Being overweight or obese
- Physical inactivity
- High sodium (salt) diet
- Low potassium diet
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Smoking and tobacco use
- Chronic stress
- Diabetes and high cholesterol

4. SYMPTOMS
------------
Most people with high blood pressure have NO symptoms, even when blood pressure is dangerously high. This is why regular monitoring is essential.

Rare symptoms at very high blood pressure levels may include:
- Severe headache
- Nausea
- Vision changes
- Nosebleeds
- Dizziness

A hypertensive crisis (blood pressure above 180/120) may cause:
- Severe chest pain
- Shortness of breath
- Severe headache
- Visual changes
- Signs of stroke

5. DIAGNOSIS
-------------
Blood pressure should be measured:
- At every healthcare visit
- At home with a home blood pressure monitor
- At pharmacy kiosks
High blood pressure is diagnosed when readings are consistently elevated over time. One high reading does not necessarily mean you have hypertension.

Proper measurement technique:
- Sit quietly for 5 minutes before measuring
- Sit with feet flat on the floor and arm supported at heart level
- Do not smoke, exercise, or drink caffeine within 30 minutes of measuring
- Take two readings 1 minute apart; record both

6. TREATMENT OPTIONS
---------------------

Lifestyle Changes (First-Line Treatment):
- Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet: Rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, low-fat dairy, and low in saturated fat and sodium
- Reduce sodium intake to less than 2,300 mg per day (less than 1 teaspoon of salt)
- Regular physical activity: At least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity exercise
- Maintain a healthy weight: Losing even 5-10 lbs can lower blood pressure
- Limit alcohol: No more than 1 drink/day for women, 2 drinks/day for men
- Quit smoking: Smoking raises blood pressure and damages blood vessels
- Manage stress: Relaxation techniques, yoga, deep breathing

Medications (When Lifestyle Changes Are Not Enough):
Common medications include:
- ACE inhibitors (lisinopril, enalapril): Relax blood vessels
- ARBs (losartan, valsartan): Relax blood vessels (alternative to ACE inhibitors)
- Calcium channel blockers (amlodipine, diltiazem): Relax blood vessel muscles
- Thiazide diuretics (hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidone): Remove excess sodium and water
- Beta blockers (metoprolol, atenolol): Slow heart rate and reduce force of heartbeat
Many patients require a combination of two or more medications.

7. HOME BLOOD PRESSURE MONITORING
------------------------------------
Monitoring at home helps track whether treatment is working.

Recommended schedule:
- Twice daily (morning and evening) when first diagnosed or changing medications
- Once daily or as directed by your provider for stable patients
- Record readings in a log or app to share with your provider

When to seek emergency care:
- Blood pressure reading above 180/120 mmHg
- Accompanied by: headache, chest pain, shortness of breath, vision changes, or signs of stroke

8. LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS IN DETAIL
---------------------------------------

The DASH Diet:
- Eat 4-5 servings of fruits per day
- Eat 4-5 servings of vegetables per day
- Choose low-fat or fat-free dairy products
- Eat whole grains instead of refined carbohydrates
- Include nuts, seeds, and legumes 4-5 times per week
- Limit red meat, sweets, and sugar-sweetened beverages

Exercise Recommendations:
- 150 minutes per week of moderate activity (brisk walking, cycling, swimming)
- 75 minutes per week of vigorous activity (jogging, aerobic classes)
- Include muscle-strengthening activities 2 days per week
- Even short walks of 10 minutes three times daily are beneficial

9. MEDICATIONS — IMPORTANT INFORMATION
-----------------------------------------
- Take medications exactly as prescribed, even if you feel fine
- Do not stop taking blood pressure medications without consulting your provider
- Blood pressure medication must be taken consistently to be effective
- Some medications may cause side effects such as dizziness when standing (especially when first starting)
- Tell your provider about ALL medications, supplements, and herbal remedies you take
- Some OTC medications (NSAIDs like ibuprofen, decongestants) can raise blood pressure

10. FOLLOW-UP CARE
--------------------
- See your provider every 1-3 months when starting treatment or adjusting medications
- Once stable, visits every 3-6 months are typically sufficient
- Blood tests to check kidney function and potassium levels are done periodically
- Your provider may adjust your medication regimen based on your response to treatment
